Cloud Operations and Management

An in-depth exploration of cloud computing principles, architectures, and advanced operational techniques for designing, deploying, and managing scalable, secure, and intelligent cloud environments

Lessons learnt and key outcomes

Across the twelve units, I developed a comprehensive understanding of cloud computing concepts, from foundational service models to advanced topics such as AI integration, serverless architecture, and hybrid cloud strategies. I strengthened my ability to design, deploy, and manage cloud infrastructures using frameworks like TOGAF and tools such as Terraform, Kubernetes, and OpenStack. Practical activities enhanced my skills in automation, scripting, security auditing, and disaster recovery planning. I also gained experience in cloud migration, compliance, and performance optimisation. Through these experiences, I learned to evaluate trade-offs in scalability, cost, and security while applying best practices. The module enabled me to build and reflect on real-world cloud solutions, preparing me for modern cloud operations and strategic decision-making.

Unit 1: Cloud Fundamentals and Service Models

This unit established my foundational understanding of cloud computing by exploring its evolution, characteristics, and service models (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS). I critically compared providers such as AWS and Google Cloud, analysing differences in flexibility, pricing transparency, and workload suitability. The discussion highlighted how architectural choices influence scalability and cost governance. A key outcome was recognising that cloud adoption is not purely technical but also strategic, requiring evaluation of trade-offs such as vendor lock-in, performance optimisation, and operational complexity across different organisational contexts.

Unit 2: Cloud Architecture and Frameworks

The focus shifted to structured cloud design through frameworks such as TOGAF, SDLC, and Infrastructure as Code. I explored how governance-driven approaches, like the ROCCA–TOGAF integration, align business strategy with cloud implementation. The unit emphasised architectural discipline, traceability, and lifecycle management. I learned that while frameworks improve consistency and compliance, they may introduce complexity and overhead. This balance between governance and agility became a key insight, reinforcing that successful cloud architecture must integrate both structured methodologies and adaptive practices in dynamic environments.

Unit 3: AI in Cloud Computing and Design Tools

This unit explored the integration of AI within cloud environments, particularly its role in automation, optimisation, and intelligent resource management. I analysed Terraform as a leading cloud design tool, highlighting its multi-cloud flexibility and compatibility with AI-driven operations. The discussions reinforced how Infrastructure as Code enhances consistency and scalability. A key takeaway was the growing importance of AIOps, where predictive analytics and automation enable self-healing and adaptive cloud systems. This unit strengthened my understanding of how AI transforms traditional cloud management into intelligent, data-driven operations.

Unit 4: Cloud Automation and Scripting

Practical skills became central in this unit through the use of scripting languages such as Bash, alongside tools like Azure CLI and OpenStack. I developed and executed automation scripts, gaining hands-on experience in provisioning and managing cloud resources. The activity demonstrated how scripting reduces manual intervention, improves repeatability, and enhances operational efficiency. I also explored GUI-based tools, understanding their usability benefits compared to command-line approaches. Overall, this unit highlighted the importance of automation as a core competency in modern cloud operations and infrastructure management.

Unit 5: Cloud-Native Technologies and Kubernetes

This week introduced cloud-native development principles, particularly the 12-Factor App methodology and container orchestration using Kubernetes. I deployed a cluster using Minikube, gaining practical insight into containerised application management. The experience emphasised scalability, portability, and resilience in cloud environments. I learned how Kubernetes automates deployment, scaling, and fault tolerance, making it essential for modern distributed systems. The key outcome was understanding how cloud-native approaches enable flexible, microservices-based architectures that can adapt to changing workloads and support continuous delivery practices.

Unit 6: Hybrid Cloud and Multi-Cloud Strategies

This unit examined hybrid cloud architectures, including fog and edge computing, and addressed challenges such as vendor lock-in. Through group work, I analysed cloud frameworks and their applicability across deployment models. The discussions reinforced the importance of interoperability and strategic flexibility when integrating on-premise and cloud systems. I learned that hybrid solutions offer performance and compliance benefits but require careful design and management. A key lesson was that multi-cloud strategies demand strong governance, cost control, and architectural planning to ensure efficiency and avoid unnecessary complexity.

Unit 7: Cloud Security and Compliance

Security became the primary focus, where I conducted a vulnerability assessment using OpenVAS on an OpenStack deployment. I identified risks such as open ports, outdated software, and weak authentication mechanisms. Implementing mitigation strategies aligned with ISO/IEC 27001 and GDPR enhanced my understanding of compliance-driven security. This unit demonstrated that cloud security requires continuous monitoring, proper configuration, and layered defence mechanisms. The key takeaway was recognising that security is not an add-on but an integral component of cloud design and operations, influencing trust and system reliability.

Unit 8: Disaster Recovery and Business Continuity

In this unit, I designed and tested a disaster recovery strategy using Duplicity for encrypted backups. By simulating a system failure and restoring services, I evaluated recovery objectives such as RTO and RPO. The exercise highlighted the importance of automation, redundancy, and regular testing in maintaining service availability. I also compared open-source solutions with managed services, understanding trade-offs in cost and efficiency. This unit reinforced that resilience planning is essential in cloud environments, ensuring minimal disruption and safeguarding organisational operations during unexpected failures.

Unit 9: Cloud Migration and Integration

The focus here was on migrating legacy systems to cloud environments while ensuring minimal downtime and data consistency. I performed a database migration using MySQL and Percona XtraBackup, reflecting on challenges such as data synchronisation and system compatibility. The unit emphasised careful planning, tool selection, and validation processes. I learned that successful migration requires balancing technical execution with business continuity considerations. Integration between cloud and on-premise systems also highlighted the complexity of hybrid environments, reinforcing the need for robust strategies and reliable migration frameworks.

Unit 10: Serverless Computing

This unit introduced serverless architecture, where infrastructure management is abstracted from developers. Using OpenFaaS, I deployed a simple function, exploring event-driven execution and scalability. The experience demonstrated how serverless reduces operational overhead and enables cost-efficient resource usage. However, I also recognised limitations such as cold start latency and reduced control over infrastructure. The key outcome was understanding how serverless fits within modern cloud architectures, particularly for microservices and lightweight applications, where rapid deployment and scalability are critical.

Unit 11: AI and Cloud Integration

This week deepened my understanding of AI-driven cloud systems through the development of an image classification model using TensorFlow and Keras. Deploying the model in a cloud environment highlighted scalability and computational advantages. I also explored ethical considerations, including data bias and limitations of AI in critical domains like healthcare. The project reinforced how AI enhances cloud operations through automation and predictive analytics. A key lesson was that while AI offers powerful capabilities, responsible implementation and validation are essential for real-world applications.

Unit 12: Future Trends in Cloud Computing

The final unit explored emerging technologies such as AI, blockchain, and quantum computing in cloud environments. I analysed how these innovations contribute to intelligent, secure, and scalable systems. Open-source tools like TensorFlow, Hyperledger, and Qiskit demonstrated the role of collaboration in driving innovation. This unit encouraged forward-thinking, highlighting the importance of adaptability in a rapidly evolving field. The key outcome was recognising that future cloud ecosystems will be increasingly autonomous, decentralised, and data-driven, requiring continuous learning and strategic awareness to remain competitive.
