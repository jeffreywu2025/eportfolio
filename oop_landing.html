Advanced Object-Oriented Design and Programming

An in-depth exploration of object-oriented programming principles and advanced software design techniques

Lessons learnt and key outcomes

By the units mentioned below, I developed a solid understanding of object-oriented programming and learned to contrast its philosophy with other programming paradigms to choose appropriate solutions for complex problems. I strengthened my ability to design object-oriented systems using UML diagrams and translate models into maintainable programs. I gained hands-on experience implementing classes, objects, inheritance, and polymorphism, while selecting suitable data structures for efficient behaviour. These techniques involved analysing requirements, creating structured designs, and applying testing, debugging, and sustainable coding practices. I also learned advanced concepts such as memory management, pointers, and modern design language evolution, enabling the use of effective design patterns. Throughout these activities, I built scalable applications in Python and Java and reflected on my progress through an e-portfolio documenting decisions and improvements.

Unit 1: Introduction and Recap of OOP

During this unit, I revisited key OOP principles—inheritance, polymorphism, abstraction, and encapsulation—while reviewing the roles of classes, objects, constructors, destructors, and access control. I also completed a practical coding exercise that involved creating class hierarchies, implementing abstract classes, applying polymorphic methods, practising encapsulation with private attributes, and experimenting with object lifecycle behaviour in Python. These activities strengthened my foundational understanding of OOP and highlighted its importance in designing scalable, maintainable, and secure software systems.

Unit 2: SOLID Principles of Object-Oriented Design

In this unit, I explored the five SOLID principles and gained an appreciation for how they support clean, maintainable, and scalable software design. I examined each principle—SRP, OCP, LSP, ISP, and DIP—and reflected on how they reduce complexity and improve extensibility in object-oriented systems. As part of the practical work, I refactored a poorly structured online shopping application in Python, separating responsibilities, introducing abstractions, and applying appropriate interfaces to address issues such as tight coupling and modification-dependent behaviours. Through this exercise, I saw how adhering to SOLID principles transforms fragile code into a robust, adaptable design that can support new features without extensive rework. This unit strengthened my understanding of high-quality software engineering practices and prepared me for more advanced design topics in later weeks.

Unit 3: Design Patterns I – Creational Patterns

This unit introduced me to the role of creational design patterns in solving object-creation problems elegantly and consistently. Rather than approaching instantiation ad hoc, I explored patterns such as Singleton, Prototype, Builder, Factory Method, and Abstract Factory to understand how they promote flexibility and reuse. For the practical activity, I designed a car manufacturing workflow where factories created different vehicle types, demonstrating how Factory Method cleanly separates creation logic from client code. This experience helped clarify how creational patterns lay the foundation for scalable software architecture.

Unit 4: Design Patterns II - Structural Patterns

My focus this week shifted toward understanding how classes and objects can be organised into larger, more adaptable structures. Structural patterns—Adapter, Bridge, Composite, Decorator, Facade, Proxy, and Flyweight—were studied through real-world examples that highlighted their usefulness in reducing complexity and enhancing modularity. The collaborative tasks encouraged me to analyse scenarios and produce Python-based demonstrations, particularly for Adapter, Bridge, and Composite patterns. I also applied the Decorator pattern in a coding exercise, experiencing firsthand how structural patterns can extend functionality without disrupting existing code.

Unit 5: Design Patterns III - Behavioural Patterns

Here, the emphasis moved from structure to interaction. Behavioural patterns such as Strategy, Observer, State, Command, Chain of Responsibility, and Template Method illustrated how objects collaborate effectively within a system. A case study on the Observer pattern in cybersecurity broadened my perspective on event-driven architecture. The collaborative discussion required me to refactor a payment-processing snippet using the Strategy pattern, replacing conditional logic with clean, interchangeable algorithms. This reinforced the idea that behavioural patterns are key to creating adaptable, communication-driven software components.

Unit 6: Concurrency and Parallelism

Concurrency became the central theme of this unit, introducing the practical realities of threads, processes, locks, and synchronisation. Instead of only learning the concepts, I confronted the challenges of race conditions and deadlocks while implementing a multithreaded banking system. Designing thread-safe methods and simulating simultaneous user transactions offered a deeper appreciation of safe shared-resource handling. Testing played a critical role, ensuring operations on the account remained consistent even under concurrent access. This unit highlighted why concurrency skills are essential for performant, modern applications.

Unit 7: Secure Coding Practices

Security took the spotlight this week, prompting me to evaluate software from the perspective of vulnerabilities rather than features. I examined weaknesses such as plaintext password storage, injection risks, weak authentication rules, and missing validation. Through the collaborative discussion, I dissected a flawed authentication system and refactored it using secure practices—password hashing, sanitisation, stronger policies, and protection against brute force. The exercise demonstrated how small oversights can lead to serious breaches, and reinforced the importance of embedding security into every stage of software design.

Unit 8: Refactoring and Code Smells

This unit sharpened my ability to spot code smells and improve software quality through structured refactoring. I learned how issues such as magic numbers and long conditional methods reduce readability, scalability, and maintainability as systems evolve. In the collaborative discussion, I analysed a pricing function and identified key smells in its hardcoded discount logic and expanding if–elif chain. I then proposed refactoring approaches, including replacing magic numbers with named constants and redesigning discount logic using polymorphism (e.g., Strategy-style classes) to support clean extension. This reinforced how refactoring reduces technical debt and sustains long-term code health.

Unit 9: Object-Oriented Software Architecture

The focus here shifted toward software architecture and its role in building scalable, maintainable, and secure systems. Instead of concentrating on individual classes, I explored architectural styles such as layered, monolithic, and microservices architectures to evaluate their trade-offs in real-world scenarios. Through a practical case study, I designed an object-oriented, layered architecture for an e-commerce platform, clearly separating presentation, business logic, and data access layers. This work highlighted how object-oriented principles and architectural decisions work together to support extensibility, security, and long-term system evolution.

Unit 10: Test-Driven Development (TDD) and BDD

The emphasis this week moved toward improving software quality through disciplined testing practices. I explored the principles of Test-Driven Development and unit testing, focusing on how writing tests first influences design decisions and reduces defects. Through a formative case study, I designed and implemented a secure e-learning platform using an object-oriented architecture, applying TDD to develop and validate core modules. Writing unit tests before implementation and refactoring based on test outcomes reinforced how testing, architecture, and design work together to produce reliable, maintainable, and secure software systems.

Unit 11: Dependency Injection and Inversion of Control (IoC)

Decoupling components became the central theme of this unit, highlighting how dependency management influences flexibility and testability in software systems. I examined how Dependency Injection and Inversion of Control invert responsibility for object creation, allowing high-level components to depend on abstractions rather than concrete implementations. In the practical exercise, I refactored a tightly coupled Python application by introducing interfaces and injecting notification services into the user management logic. This approach enabled effective unit testing through mocking and simplified the addition of new services, demonstrating how DI and IoC contribute to scalable, maintainable architecture.

Unit 12: Recent Developments and Review

The final unit brought together the full scope of learning from across the module, culminating in the completion of a comprehensive e-Portfolio and Capstone Project. This stage focused on consolidating advanced object-oriented design, software architecture, secure coding, testing, and design patterns through structured reflection and applied examples. By revisiting practical artefacts, case studies, and coding exercises, I evaluated how these concepts were used to address real-world problems in areas such as security, scalability, and maintainability. The reflective process also encouraged critical assessment of my academic growth, technical strengths, and future development goals, reinforcing readiness for advanced software design challenges beyond the course.
